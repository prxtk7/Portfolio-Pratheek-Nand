#  Exercise 5: CNC Milling – Wooden Candle Holder



##  Overview
This exercise offered a practical introduction to the end-to-end digital fabrication workflow, tracing a design's progression from a 2D vector drawing to a finished physical product.

The process kicked off with an instructional laboratory workshop where the professor demonstrated the CNC milling machine and explained the interplay between software configuration, machine parameters, and material execution. Witnessing the complete fabrication cycle firsthand underscored the critical importance of dimensional precision before drafting my custom candle holder in Inkscape according to the specified project parameters.

Once the vector files were reviewed, approved, and fabricated on the CNC mill, evaluating the final wooden product provided a valuable opportunity to compare digital intent against the physical outcome, effectively bridging the gap between design and manufacturing.


---


###  Design Process

For this exercise, I designed a ninja shuriken-shaped candle holder, aiming for a striking geometric aesthetic for the final wood product. While drafting the vector layout, I carefully followed the project parameters incorporating a central circular pocket with a precise 39.5 mm diameter.

Although my initial focus was primarily on the visual composition of the shuriken profile, the design review highlighted that the circular candle pocket required tighter dimensional accuracy to satisfy the technical constraints of the CNC mill. I adjusted the vector geometry based on this feedback before final submission.

This iteration underscored a vital manufacturing lesson: successful CNC fabrication relies entirely on the precision of the digital drawing, meaning even minor dimensional discrepancies must be resolved before machining begins.

###  CNC Milling Workshop

Prior to fabrication, I participated in a CNC milling workshop that demonstrated the practical execution of a machining cycle. I observed how the wooden workpiece is rigidly fixtured, how feed rates and spindle speeds are configured, and how the cutting tool translates a digital toolpath into precise material removal.

While this was a supervised demonstration rather than direct operation, witnessing the physical execution of a vector file provided critical insight into industrial workflows. The experience clearly highlighted that successful manufacturing depends just as much on proper machine calibration and material selection as it does on flawless file preparation.

###  CNC Fabricated Output

Receiving the finished shuriken candle holder a few days later was highly rewarding, as the physical object precisely mirrored the vector layout I had drafted. I selected a lighter, natural wood stock to highlight its clean grain pattern and smooth surface finish. Examining the finalized piece provided a strong sense of accomplishment, showcasing a flawless transition from a flat digital drawing to a tangible, machined product.

Ultimately, comparing the physical outcome against my original file reinforced a core manufacturing lesson: the aesthetic and structural quality of a finished product is entirely dictated by the decisions made during the initial design phase.

###  Observations
- CNC milling is fundamentally dependent on the quality of the initial file. While the physical machining is automated, the final product's accuracy is entirely determined by the precision of the input data.
- The feedback received during the review phase emphasized that exact dimensional tolerances are essential before manufacturing. Resolving minor geometry discrepancies early is crucial to preventing downstream production errors.
- The final product demonstrated the exceptional replication accuracy of the CNC mill, proving that meticulous digital preparation directly results in a flawless physical translation.

This exercise provided a comprehensive introduction to digital fabrication by tracing the complete computer-controlled manufacturing cycle from the initial workshop demonstration to the final machining of my design. Ultimately, bridging the gap between CAD modeling and CAM execution strengthened my proficiency in vector-based layouts and established the fundamental confidence required to engineer production-ready digital files.
