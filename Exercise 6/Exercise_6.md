#  Exercise 6: Laser Cut Business Card



##  Overview
This exercise explored the end-to-end laser fabrication workflow by designing and producing a professional academic business card using Inkscape and an Epilog laser cutter. I engineered a clean, minimalist layout optimized for physical execution following an instructional workshop covering machine safety and material selection. The following report details the design iterations, machine configurations, and key technical insights gained from bridging digital drafting with physical manufacturing.


---


###  Design Process

The business card was drafted in Inkscape following the structural workflow introduced during the workshop. I focused the layout entirely on essential academic details—including my name, master's programme, and university email—intentionally keeping the design minimalist to ensure the text remained legible after laser engraving.

Throughout the drafting process, I made minor adjustments to the alignment and spatial distribution of the elements to optimise visual balance before preparing the final file for production. To ensure optimal material processing, the parameters were split into distinct operational settings: the Raster Setting for engraving was configured at 60% speed and 100% power, while the Vector Setting for cutting the perimeter was set to a precise 7% speed and 100% power.

###  Laser Fabrication

Once the design was complete, the artwork was exported according to the workshop requirements and prepared for fabrication. I selected the wood-finish cardboard from the available materials, as its organic texture complemented the card's minimalist aesthetic.

Under the professor's supervision, I observed the complete manufacturing cycle as the laser cutter engraved the text and graphic elements before cleanly cutting the final perimeter. Watching the machine accurately execute the digital file demonstrated the high precision of laser fabrication, reinforcing how directly the quality and accuracy of a physical product depend on a meticulously prepared vector layout.

###  Design Verification & Outcome

Upon receiving the finalized business card, the physical execution proved highly successful, yielding sharp, perfectly legible engraved text. Comparing the completed card against the original Inkscape layout confirmed that the physical product directly mirrored my digital intent. The synergy between the clean composition and the organic texture of the cardboard delivered a highly professional finish, fully validating the minimalist design approach planned from the outset.

###  Observations
- This exercise proved that laser-cut quality depends entirely on digital design preparation; subtle layout and spacing adjustments directly yielded a crisper engraving.
- Observing the fabrication process demonstrated how flawlessly the machine translates properly optimized vector graphics into physical geometry.
- Material selection heavily influenced the final aesthetic, with the wood-finish cardboard providing a refined texture that elevated the minimalist layout.
- The project underscored the necessity of strict adherence to established fabrication workflows and lab safety protocols prior to machine operation.


---


##  Conclusions
This exercise offered invaluable hands-on experience across the entire laser fabrication workflow, bridging the gap between digital vector drafting and precise physical manufacturing. Designing the business card forced me to think holistically, balancing layout and legibility to ensure the final product was both functional and aesthetically refined. Ultimately, witnessing the machine accurately translate my digital file into a clean, physical object has significantly built my technical confidence in engineering production-ready assets for laser fabrication.
