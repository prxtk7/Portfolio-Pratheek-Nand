#  Exercise 4: E-Textiles — LED Patch



##  Overview
In this exercise, we designed and hand-crafted an e-textile patch suitable for garment integration, constructing a soft circuit directly embedded into the fabric using conductive thread, sewable LEDs, and a 3V coin-cell battery holder. This project provided valuable, hands-on experience with functional materials and soft circuit architecture.


---


###  Patch Design

I crafted a custom patch featuring a distinct, house-like silhouette—combining a clean rectangular body with a sharp, triangular pointed top. I gathered the materials—fabric, conductive thread, sewable LEDs, a coin-cell battery holder, and sewing tools—and tested the electronics to make sure everything worked perfectly. As a beginner to e-textiles, the first challenge was simply deciding what to make. I wanted a simple, approachable project that would let me focus on mastering how fabric and electronics interact.

To bring the idea to life, I sketched out a clean, house-like shape combining a rectangular base with a pointed top, planning exactly where each component would sit. Once the layout was set, I used it as a template to cut my ribbed base fabric and a matching cover piece, transferring the final design directly onto the textile layers.

###  Circuit Construction

With the design finalized and the house-shaped fabric cut, I transitioned to assembling the hardware components. I positioned the purple coin-cell battery holder right in the center of the patch to act as the main hub, and arranged five sewable LEDs around the perimeter—placing one at the very peak of the roof, two at the lower roof corners, and two at the bottom base corners to trace the outline of the silhouette.

I carefully stitched the components down using conductive thread. I ran distinct thread traces along the edges to link the positive and negative terminals of the LEDs back to the central power source, ensuring the paths remained clear and short-circuit free. This assembly phase really highlighted how unique soft circuits are compared to rigid electronics; every stitch pulled double duty, simultaneously securing the hardware to the fabric and forming the literal wiring of the system.

###  Testing and Troubleshooting

Upon inserting the coin-cell battery, only a portion of the LEDs initially illuminated. A detailed inspection and consultation with my supervisor revealed that overlapping positive and negative thread pathways were causing a short circuit.

To resolve this, I rerouted the lines to ensure complete separation throughout the layout. Following these adjustments, a subsequent test confirmed that all five components functioned successfully, with the two roof-corner LEDs illuminating in blue and the remaining three at the peak and base illuminating in red to complete the silhouette.

###  Final Layering and Aesthetics

Once the circuit was fully operational, I integrated the matching cover layer over the electronic hardware. The addition of this second textile piece successfully concealed the raw conductive stitching and central battery holder, allowing the blue and red LEDs to diffuse cleanly through the fabric while preserving the clean, house-shaped silhouette. This step transformed the prototype from an exposed circuit into a polished, completed textile artifact.

###  Observations
- Working with conductive thread requires significantly more precision and strategic planning than traditional wire-based electronics.
- Because the uninsulated thread is flexible, soft circuits are highly susceptible to short circuits if the routing pathways are not meticulously mapped out from the start.
- Troubleshooting and isolating electrical errors becomes increasingly difficult once multiple textile layers are assembled, making frequent testing throughout the construction phase critical.
- This process demonstrated the viability of textiles as functional, integrated electronic mediums, shifting their role from purely structural or decorative materials into active hardware components.

##  Conclusion
By seamlessly bridging fabric and hardware, this project transformed a simple textile into an active electronic interface, laying a strong foundation for the future of interactive wearable systems.
